Abstract

This thesis investigates how individualism relates to stock market participation by exploring mediating factors such as social conformity, overconfidence and optimism, financial literacy, and trust. Using survey data, the study tests five hypotheses to assess the comprehensive effect of individualism by identifying both its direct and indirect effects on stock market participation. Results show a significant positive relationship between individualism and overconfidence, optimism, and institutional trust, while there is a negative relationship with generalized trust. No significant relationships were found between individualism and either social conformity or financial literacy. While analyses suggest a modest positive relationship between individualism and stock market participation, this relationship is found to be statistically inconclusive. Mediation analysis indicates overconfidence serves as the primary pathway through which individualism may influence stock market participation.

Research Background

For decades, economists have puzzled over why many individuals avoid stock market investments despite compelling theoretical models suggesting they should participate. This disparity—known as the "limited stock market participation puzzle"—has profound implications for individual wealth accumulation and challenges established economic theory.

Traditional research focused on socioeconomic and institutional factors like entry costs, risk aversion, wealth, and education. More recent studies have expanded to include behavioral factors such as social conformity, trust, overconfidence, and financial literacy. However, these factors alone cannot explain systematic differences in participation rates across countries and among similar demographics.

Culture—particularly the dimension of individualism—has emerged as a critical factor potentially explaining these differences. Previous research has linked individualism to financial literacy, trust, social conformity, and overconfidence, all factors that may influence stock market participation.

Research Contribution

This thesis addresses inconsistencies in existing literature by examining the comprehensive effect of individualism on stock market participation (SMP), integrating key behavioral and psychological factors:

  • Social conformity
  • Overconfidence and optimism
  • Financial literacy
  • Trust (both generalized and institutional)

The study employed a survey with 150 U.S. participants, equally divided between control and treatment groups. While the treatment was designed to prime individualism, it did not produce statistically significant changes in individualism measures, leading to a shift in analytical approach toward examining direct relationships between individualism and key variables.

Key Findings

  • Individualism shows a significant positive relationship with overconfidence and optimism. A one-unit increase in individualism was associated with a 0.083 standard deviation increase in overconfidence (p = 0.035). It also led to a 0.14 unit increase in optimism (p = 0.004).
  • Individualism has opposing effects on different dimensions of trust:
    • Negative relationship with generalized trust (-0.067, p = 0.014).
    • Positive relationship with institutional trust (+0.073, p = 0.027).
  • No significant relationships were found between individualism and either social conformity or financial literacy.
  • Although analyses suggest a positive relationship between individualism and stock market participation, most results lacked statistical significance.
  • The mediation analysis revealed that individualism's effect on stock market participation primarily operates through the pathway of overconfidence.

The research suggests that overconfidence serves as the primary mediator through which individualism may influence stock market participation, providing new insights into the "limited stock market participation puzzle" while highlighting the complex interplay between cultural dimensions and financial decision-making.

Research Design

  • Survey Method: Two surveys administered on Prolific
  • Sample Size: 150 participants total
  • Sampling: Equal distribution between control and treatment groups
  • Participant Criteria: U.S. residents with American nationality, fluent in English
  • Timing: Both surveys conducted simultaneously on February 19, 2025

Experimental Conditions

  • Control Group: Standard survey measuring key variables
  • Treatment Group: Identical survey plus a "pronoun circling task" designed to prime individualism
  • Treatment Task: Modified version of the pronoun circling task where participants counted singular pronouns in a paragraph containing both singular and plural pronouns

Key Variables Measured

1. Trust
  • Generalized trust (trust in other people) - using Rosenberg's 'Trust in People' scale
  • Institutional trust (confidence in institutions) - using Likert scale measurements
2. Financial Literacy
  • Modified version of Lusardi and Mitchell's 'Big Three' questions
  • Simplified assessment of basic concepts: simple interest, compound interest, and inflation
  • Modified OECD survey questions
3. Overconfidence and Optimism
  • Better-than-average effect (using Zaiane and Abaoub's questions)
  • Stated confidence (self-reported beliefs about correctness of financial literacy answers)
  • Optimism about stock market returns
4. Social Conformity
  • Two items from Mehrabian and Stefl's Conformity Scale
5. Individualism/Collectivism
  • Comprehensive measurement incorporating items from multiple established scales:
    • Singelis (1994)
    • Triandis & Gelfand (1998)
    • Shulruf et al. (2007)
    • Chen & West (2008)
6. Stock Market Participation
  • Measured through direct ownership of stocks
  • Binary indicator (yes/no)

This methodology allowed for examination of how individualism relates to stock market participation through various mediating factors.
